Introduction

Densely populated urban areas in developing countries often lack any kind of data that would enable the monitoring systems. This is the situation of many informal settlements (slum areas) in the world including our area of study: Kibera Slum - Kenya. In our project we used satellite pictures to delineate building roofs in order to count them and estimate the number of slum population. Furthermore, the spatial data generated by our approach can help the mapping of vulnerable areas to local disasters, such as floods, fires or conflicts.

The GAP

Detection of individual roof buildings from informal settlements using satellite imagery is especially challenging task due to the microstructure, merged/overlapping rooftops and irregular shapes of buildings in slum-like areas. High spatial resolution is essential to facilitate extraction of individual buildings that are characterized by small, densely packed shanties and other structures (Veljanovski, T., et al. (2012).
Our area of study Kibera is composed of varying sizes of houses, where roofs can be a combination of many different materials. Typically this can produce a spectral response on satellite imagery that is difficult to interpret and makes it difficult for traditional classification strategies to differentiate the building roofs.
